## What makes a good card

- As a good default, each card should teach exactly one idea.
- The rare exception is if a specific combination of two ideas or pieces of knowledge very commonly co-occur or can best be understood jointly.
- Cards should include just enough scaffold to help the brain retrieve the right information without a pure guess, but not so much context that it becomes a simple test of recognition instead of recall.
- One way to avoid skipping straight to the answer is fill-in-the-blank style questions (cloze deletion cards).
- Try to include, in addition to specifics, a few "atomic" concept cards. An "atomic" concept is one that is a distinct, reusable idea that solves a class of problems or set of questions (not just specific ones), that ideally can be combined with other atomic concepts, and will be frequently useful.
- Imagery can occasionally be leveraged to great effect, especially in anatomy, geography, geometry, chemistry, history, and more — however, do not add image-based content to cards unless one already exists.
- There are a few mnemonic techniques that can be very helpful, but shouldn't be overused or used purely as a crutch.
- Although AI cannot help with this, personalization in references can enhance memories.
- As long as they are not overused or don't interfere with other cards, illustrating with emotional states or examples that are vivid, even shocking, can be helpful.
- In cards that invoke reasoning, the card ideally should prompt/cue the same reasoning path instead of simply being memorized, though this isn't always possible. For instance, prompt an individual derivation step.
- The same knowledge represented from multiple different angles in different cards can be helpful for high-value concepts.

## Common pitfalls

- **Long, overstuffed cards** that cannot be read quickly increase errors and reduce specificity. Keep cards concise.
- **Too many concepts at once** — multiple facts on one card will conflict and interfere with each other in memory.
- **Interference between cards** — avoid cards that could accidentally overwrite each other in the brain. Keep things unambiguous and apply the minimum information principle.
- **Lists or giant sets** are bad on one card, in part because it's unclear where to categorize the card if only some items are recalled.
- Usually an overpacked card is best split into multiple cards, which helps repetition and the scheduling algorithm work better. Occasionally, where items naturally build on each other, a multiple-blank cloze card may be a good solution.
- When making a set of cloze deletion cards, it is perfectly acceptable to "overlap" information between cards — similar statements leaving different blanks — if both pieces are important on their own. This overlap can actually strengthen memory.
- If it is absolutely necessary to create a "set" card, favor enumerations ordered in a rigid, always-consistent way, and rarely go beyond 5 items.
- **Sources can differ.** If a fact or figure will vary, include the source in the answer but not as something that must be part of the learned knowledge itself. If something could change in the future, date-stamp the piece of information.

## Style

When editing a card, preserve the original flavor when possible, including the user's original phrasing or framing, unless it conflicts with a more well-established principle. Default to simple, readable Markdown, occasionally using **bold** where appropriate, but do not bold too many things on one card side.
